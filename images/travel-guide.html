Travel Essentials

Before setting foot in Nepal, there are a few essential things you should know to ensure a smooth and enjoyable journey.

Visa & Entry Requirements

+

Most foreigners need a visa to enter Nepal. You can obtain one upon arrival at Tribhuvan International Airport or at land border crossings.

  • Tourist Visa Fees:
    • 15 days: $30 USD
    • 30 days: $50 USD
    • 90 days: $125 USD
  • Required Documents:
    • Valid passport (minimum 6 months validity)
    • Completed visa application form (available online or at arrival)
    • Passport-sized photo
    • Visa fee in cash (USD, EUR, GBP accepted)
  • Online Application: You can fill out the form before arrival at the Department of Immigration website

Citizens of SAARC countries (except Afghanistan) don't need a visa to enter Nepal.

Best Time to Visit

+

Nepal has four main seasons, each offering unique experiences:

  • Spring (March to May): Warm temperatures, clear skies, wildflowers in bloom. Great for trekking and mountain views.
  • Summer/Monsoon (June to August): Hot and rainy. Not ideal for trekking, but perfect for rain-shadow areas like Upper Mustang and cultural experiences.
  • Autumn (September to November): Clear skies, mild temperatures, and excellent visibility. The most popular season for tourists.
  • Winter (December to February): Cold at higher elevations but mild in lower regions. Great for lower-altitude trekking and fewer crowds.

Recommendation: Autumn (October-November) offers the best weather for most activities.

SIM Cards & Internet

+

Staying connected in Nepal is relatively easy and affordable:

  • Major Providers: Ncell and Nepal Telecom (NTC) offer the best coverage
  • Where to Buy: SIM cards are available at the Tribhuvan International Airport or at authorized stores in cities (bring your passport)
  • Costs:
    • SIM Card: 100-300 NPR (approx. $1-3 USD)
    • Data Packages: 500 NPR (approx. $4 USD) for 5-10GB depending on provider
  • Coverage: Good in major cities and tourist areas; limited in remote trekking regions
  • Wi-Fi: Available in most hotels, restaurants, and cafes in urban areas

Tip: Ncell generally has better data speeds in urban areas, while NTC has wider coverage in remote regions.

Currency & Money

+

The official currency is the Nepalese Rupee (NPR). Managing your money in Nepal:

  • Exchange Rates: Check current rates before you travel (approximately 1 USD = 130 NPR, but rates fluctuate)
  • Where to Exchange:
    • Banks and authorized money changers offer the best rates
    • Major hotels can exchange currency but at lower rates
    • ATMs are widely available in cities and tourist areas
  • ATMs: Most accept Visa and Mastercard, with withdrawal limits of 10,000-35,000 NPR per transaction
  • Credit Cards: Accepted in high-end establishments but cash is preferred for small businesses, especially outside major cities
  • Tipping: Not mandatory but appreciated (10% is standard in restaurants if service charge isn't included)

Note: Always carry some cash, especially when traveling to remote areas. It's illegal to exchange currency with unauthorized individuals.

Packing Essentials

+

What to bring depends on your activities and the season, but here are some essentials:

  • Documents:
    • Passport and visa
    • Travel insurance details
    • Printed hotel reservations
    • Emergency contacts
  • Clothing:
    • Lightweight, breathable clothes for lower elevations
    • Warm layers for higher altitudes
    • Waterproof jacket (essential in monsoon season)
    • Comfortable walking shoes or hiking boots
    • Modest clothing for temple visits (covering shoulders and knees)
  • Health Items:
    • Basic first aid kit
    • Prescription medications
    • Sunscreen and insect repellent
    • Hand sanitizer and wet wipes
    • Water purification tablets or filter
  • Electronics:
    • Universal power adapter (Nepal uses Type C, D, and M plugs)
    • Portable power bank
    • Headlamp or flashlight (power outages can occur)

Tip: Most trekking gear can be purchased or rented in Kathmandu and Pokhara at reasonable prices if you don't want to bring everything from home.

Where to Stay

Nepal offers a wide range of accommodation options to suit every budget and preference. From luxury hotels to homestays, here's what you need to know:

Luxury Hotels

Experience world-class hospitality with stunning views of the Himalayas. Major cities like Kathmandu and Pokhara offer 5-star accommodations with all modern amenities.

Price Range: $100-300+ USD per night

Budget Guesthouses

Simple, clean rooms with basic amenities. Very common in tourist areas and provide excellent value for money.

Price Range: $5-30 USD per night

Best For: Backpackers, long-term travelers

Teahouses (Trekking)

Basic accommodation along trekking routes providing meals and shelter. A quintessential part of the Nepal trekking experience.

Price Range: $3-15 USD per night

Best For: Trekkers, adventure seekers

Note: Facilities become more basic as altitude increases. Advance booking is recommended during peak season.

Homestays

Live with local families and experience authentic Nepali hospitality and culture. Available in rural areas and some urban neighborhoods.

Price Range: $15-40 USD per night (often including meals)

Best For: Cultural immersion, authentic experiences

Monastery Stays

A unique opportunity to stay in Buddhist monasteries and experience spiritual life. Basic accommodations with cultural significance.

Price Range: Donation-based or $5-15 USD per night

Best For: Spiritual seekers, meditation practitioners

Note: Respect monastery rules and schedules. Not all monasteries accept guests; inquire in advance.

Accommodation Tips:

  • Book in advance during peak seasons (October-November and March-April)
  • Many places in Kathmandu and Pokhara offer free airport pickup
  • Power outages can occur; check if accommodations have backup generators
  • Hot water availability varies, especially in budget accommodations
  • Wi-Fi quality can be inconsistent; check reviews if this is important to you
  • Negotiating prices is acceptable in budget accommodations, especially for longer stays

Nepali Cuisine & Where to Eat

Nepali cuisine is diverse, flavorful, and heavily influenced by its neighbors India and Tibet, yet maintains its unique identity. Here's your guide to eating in Nepal:

Must-Try Dishes

  • Dal Bhat: The national dish - rice, lentil soup, and curry with vegetables or meat
  • Momos: Steamed or fried dumplings filled with vegetables or meat
  • Sel Roti: Sweet rice bread, often served during festivals
  • Thukpa: Noodle soup popular in mountainous regions
  • Chatamari: Newari rice flour "pizza" with various toppings
  • Yomari: Sweet dumplings filled with molasses or sesame
Learn More

Where to Eat

  • Local Restaurants: Authentic food at reasonable prices
  • Tourist Restaurants: International menus with higher prices
  • Street Food Stalls: Inexpensive, authentic flavors
  • Food Courts: Found in shopping malls with variety
  • Hotel Restaurants: Safe but pricier options

Recommended Areas:

Kathmandu: Thamel, Jhamsikhel, Durbar Marg
Pokhara: Lakeside, Old Bazaar
Bhaktapur: Durbar Square area

Food Safety Tips

  • Drink only bottled, filtered, or purified water
  • Ensure meat is thoroughly cooked
  • Be cautious with street food - choose busy stalls with high turnover
  • Avoid raw vegetables and fruits unless you can peel them yourself
  • Check if the restaurant uses filtered water for preparation
  • Popular establishments with many customers generally serve fresher food

Dietary Restrictions

  • Vegetarians: Well catered for throughout Nepal
  • Vegans: Options available in tourist areas, but clarify about ghee (clarified butter) use
  • Gluten-Free: Rice-based cuisine is naturally gluten-free, but soy sauce may contain gluten
  • Halal: Available in some areas, especially in Kathmandu

Key Phrases for Dietary Restrictions:

"Ma shakahari hu" (I am vegetarian)
"Maile masu khaadina" (I don't eat meat)

Nepali Dining Etiquette:

  • Traditionally, food is eaten with the right hand
  • Wash hands before and after meals
  • It's considered polite to finish all the food on your plate
  • When invited to someone's home, wait to be told where to sit
  • Don't pass food with your left hand
  • Saying "Dhanybhad" (thank you) after a meal is appreciated

Must-Visit Destinations

Nepal is incredibly diverse, offering everything from the world's highest mountains to subtropical jungles. Here are some destinations you shouldn't miss:

Kathmandu Valley

Nepal's capital and cultural heart featuring UNESCO World Heritage sites, ancient temples, and vibrant markets.

  • Must See: Durbar Square, Swayambhunath (Monkey Temple), Pashupatinath, Boudhanath
  • Days Needed: 3-4 days
  • Best For: History, architecture, culture, shopping

Pokhara

Lakeside paradise with stunning Annapurna mountain views and gateway to popular treks.

  • Must See: Phewa Lake, World Peace Pagoda, Sarangkot sunrise, Davis Falls
  • Days Needed: 2-4 days
  • Best For: Relaxation, adventure sports, pre/post-trekking rest

Everest Region

Home to the world's highest peak and the famous Everest Base Camp trek.

  • Must See: Namche Bazaar, Tengboche Monastery, Kala Patthar, Gokyo Lakes
  • Days Needed: 12-16 days for EBC trek
  • Best For: Trekking, mountain views, Sherpa culture
Explore Everest Region

Annapurna Region

One of the world's greatest treks featuring diverse landscapes and cultures.

  • Must See: Thorong La Pass, Poon Hill, Tilicho Lake, Marpha Village
  • Days Needed: 10-21 days for full circuit, 7-10 days for sanctuary
  • Best For: Trekking with tea house comfort, diverse landscapes

Chitwan National Park

UNESCO-listed wildlife reserve offering safaris and a chance to see rhinos, tigers, and more.

  • Must See: Jungle safaris, Tharu cultural shows, canoeing on Rapti River
  • Days Needed: 2-3 days
  • Best For: Wildlife, nature, relaxation
Explore Chitwan

Lumbini

Birthplace of Lord Buddha and an important pilgrimage site for Buddhists worldwide.

  • Must See: Maya Devi Temple, Ashoka Pillar, Peace Pagoda, international monasteries
  • Days Needed: 1-2 days
  • Best For: Religious history, meditation, spiritual journey
Explore Lumbini

Off the Beaten Path:

  • Upper Mustang: Former forbidden kingdom with Tibetan culture
  • Rara Lake: Nepal's largest lake in remote western region
  • Langtang Valley: Less crowded trekking with amazing mountain views
  • Bardia National Park: Wilder and less visited alternative to Chitwan
  • Ilam: Tea gardens and rolling hills in eastern Nepal

Cultural Guide

Nepal is a cultural mosaic with over 125 ethnic groups and 123 languages. Understanding local customs will enrich your experience:

Customs & Etiquette

+
  • Greetings: Place palms together in front of chest and say "Namaste" with a slight bow
  • Shoes: Remove before entering homes, temples, and sometimes shops
  • Pointing: Avoid pointing with your finger or feet at people or religious objects
  • Head: The head is considered sacred - avoid touching others' heads
  • Public Display of Affection: Conservative culture - minimal PDA is appreciated
  • Photography: Always ask permission before photographing people
  • Haggling: Expected in markets and with taxis, but keep it respectful
  • Trekking Etiquette: Always pass others on the mountain side of trails and yield to porters/yaks

Religious Practices

+

Nepal is primarily Hindu and Buddhist, with practices often blending together:

  • Temple Visits:
    • Remove shoes before entering
    • Walk clockwise around stupas and temples
    • Some Hindu temples restrict non-Hindu visitors
    • Avoid touching offerings or religious objects
  • Prayer Flags: Colorful flags with prayers that spread blessings through the wind
  • Prayer Wheels: Spin clockwise while walking past
  • Morning Rituals: Many Nepalis perform puja (worship) in the early morning
  • Sadhu Etiquette: Holy men may request money for photos - this is customary

Hinduism in Nepal

Nepal was the world's only Hindu kingdom until 2008. Hinduism here has unique features:

  • Living Goddess Kumari tradition
  • Pashupatinath - one of the most sacred Shiva temples
  • Animal sacrifice during Dashain festival
  • Unique blend with Buddhism and animist traditions

Buddhism in Nepal

Buddha was born in Lumbini, Nepal, and Buddhism remains vibrant:

  • Tibetan Buddhist traditions in mountain regions
  • Newari Buddhism in Kathmandu Valley
  • Ancient monasteries and stupas
  • Meditation retreats available for visitors

Festivals

+

Nepal celebrates numerous festivals throughout the year:

  • Dashain (September/October): The most important Hindu festival lasting 15 days
  • Tihar/Diwali (October/November): Festival of lights honoring Laxmi, goddess of wealth
  • Holi (February/March): Colorful spring festival where people throw colored powder
  • Losar (February): Tibetan New Year celebrated in mountainous regions
  • Indra Jatra (September): Major Newari festival in Kathmandu with chariot processions
  • Buddha Jayanti (May): Buddha's birthday celebrated at Buddhist sites
  • Mani Rimdu (November): Sherpa festival with masked dances in Everest region

Tip: Festival dates change yearly as they follow lunar calendars. Check current dates before planning your trip.

Practical Information

Essential information to help you navigate day-to-day life in Nepal:

Transportation

+

Getting Around Cities:

  • Taxis: Abundant in cities, negotiate fare before starting or insist on meter
  • Rickshaws: Good for short distances in historic areas
  • Local Buses: Inexpensive but often crowded and challenging to navigate
  • Ride Apps: Pathao and Tootle are popular in Kathmandu
  • Rental Motorcycles/Scooters: Available in tourist areas (international license required)

Intercity Travel:

  • Tourist Buses: Comfortable options between major destinations
  • Local Buses: Reach remote areas but can be uncomfortable and slow
  • Domestic Flights: Time-saving options to major destinations
  • Jeeps: Private hire for rough roads or remote areas

Tips:

  • Book domestic flights in advance during peak seasons
  • Allow extra time for all journeys due to road conditions
  • Tourist buses are generally safer and more comfortable than local buses
  • Motion sickness remedies are recommended for mountain roads

Electricity

+
  • Voltage: 230V AC, 50Hz
  • Plugs: Type C (European 2-pin), Type D (Indian 3-pin), and Type M
  • Power Cuts: Still occur in some areas though less frequent than in past years
  • Adapters: Universal adapters are recommended
  • Charging: Power banks are useful for trekking areas

Shopping

+

What to Buy:

  • Pashmina shawls and cashmere products
  • Handmade paper products
  • Thangka paintings
  • Singing bowls
  • Handicrafts and woodcarvings
  • Prayer flags and wheels
  • Nepali tea
  • Khukuri knives (check import regulations for your home country)

Shopping Tips:

  • Haggling is expected in markets and small shops (not in fixed-price stores)
  • Start at around 40-50% of the initial asking price
  • Be respectful and good-humored when negotiating
  • Quality varies widely - inspect items carefully
  • Support fair trade shops where possible

Best Shopping Areas:

  • Thamel in Kathmandu for souvenirs and trekking gear
  • Patan for metalwork and handicrafts
  • Bhaktapur for pottery and woodcarvings
  • Lakeside in Pokhara for casual shopping

Tipping

+
  • Restaurants: 5-10% if service charge isn't included
  • Hotels: NPR 50-100 for porters, NPR 100-200 per day for housekeeping
  • Taxis: Rounding up the fare is sufficient
  • Guides: USD 7-15 per day
  • Porters: USD 5-8 per day
  • Trekking Staff: Tip at the end of the trek, usually in a ceremony

Note: These are guidelines. Tip according to service quality and your budget.

Safety & Health

Nepal is generally a safe country for travelers, but it's wise to take precautions:

General Safety

+
  • Keep valuables secure and be mindful in crowded areas
  • Use registered taxis, especially at night
  • Be cautious around stray dogs and monkeys
  • Avoid political demonstrations
  • Register with your embassy if staying for an extended period
  • Solo female travelers should exercise standard precautions
  • Trust your instincts and be aware of common scams

Common Scams:

  • Fake trekking agencies or guides
  • Gem and antique scams
  • "Closed" attractions redirecting to shops
  • Overcharging for taxi rides

Tip: Book trekking agencies through official sources like the Trekking Agencies' Association of Nepal (TAAN).

Trekking Safety

+
  • Always trek with a partner or group
  • Register for Trekkers' Information Management System (TIMS) card
  • Purchase appropriate travel insurance that covers high-altitude trekking and evacuation
  • Acclimatize properly to avoid altitude sickness
  • Carry a first aid kit and know how to use it
  • Stay hydrated and maintain energy levels
  • Inform someone of your itinerary
  • Check weather forecasts before setting out
  • Be prepared for sudden weather changes
  • Don't trek alone in remote areas

Health Precautions

+

Before Travel:

  • Consult your doctor about vaccinations (typically Hepatitis A, Typhoid, and others)
  • Consider a dental check-up
  • Prepare a comprehensive first aid kit
  • Bring prescription medications with doctor's note
  • Purchase travel insurance with medical coverage and evacuation

During Travel:

  • Drink only bottled or purified water
  • Avoid ice in drinks unless you're sure it's made from purified water
  • Eat thoroughly cooked food
  • Wash hands frequently
  • Use sunscreen and insect repellent
  • Stay hydrated, especially when trekking

Altitude Sickness:

  • Ascend gradually (no more than 300-500m per day above 3,000m)
  • Stay hydrated and avoid alcohol
  • Consider preventive medication like Diamox (consult your doctor)
  • Know the symptoms: headache, nausea, dizziness, fatigue, loss of appetite
  • If symptoms persist, descend immediately
  • "Climb high, sleep low" principle

Language Tips:

  • In remote areas, local dialects may differ from standard Nepali
