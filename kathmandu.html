Discover the ancient heart of Nepal's capital city, where history, art, and spirituality converge in a mesmerizing tapestry of culture

Kathmandu: The City of Temples

Ancient Valley of Wonders

Nestled in a valley surrounded by the Himalayan mountains, Kathmandu is Nepal's capital and largest city. With a history spanning over 2,000 years, it stands as one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world.

The name "Kathmandu" derives from "Kasthamandap," which means "wooden shelter" in Sanskrit, referring to a pagoda built from a single tree that once stood in Durbar Square. Today, the city serves as the cultural, economic, and political center of Nepal.

Kathmandu is known as the "City of Temples" due to its abundance of religious structures, with Hindu and Buddhist influences coexisting harmoniously throughout the valley. The cityscape is dotted with ancient stupas, pagodas, and shikhara-style temples that reflect the rich spiritual heritage of the region.

Durbar Square: Royal Legacy

The Ancient Royal Plaza

Kathmandu Durbar Square, locally known as "Hanuman Dhoka," was the royal palace of the Malla kings and later the Shah dynasty who ruled over the city. "Durbar" means "palace" or "court," reflecting its historical significance as the seat of Nepalese royalty.

This UNESCO World Heritage Site is a testament to the architectural and artistic brilliance of the Newari craftsmen who shaped the valley's distinctive aesthetic. The complex houses a remarkable collection of palaces, courtyards, and temples built between the 12th and 18th centuries.

Despite suffering damage during the devastating 2015 earthquake, significant restoration work has returned much of the square to its former glory, preserving this irreplaceable piece of Nepal's cultural heritage for future generations.

Architectural Marvel

Newari Craftsmanship

The architecture of Durbar Square exemplifies the Newari style, characterized by intricate wood and stone carvings, multi-tiered pagoda roofs, and ornate windows. Each structure tells a story through its detailed craftsmanship.

The buildings showcase a remarkable fusion of Hindu and Buddhist artistic elements, reflecting Nepal's unique religious syncretism. Elaborate strut brackets, carved doorways, and windows adorned with deities demonstrate the extraordinary skill of medieval craftsmen.

The tiered temples, with their diminishing levels representing the cosmic mountain of Mount Meru, are not only visually striking but also symbolically significant in Hindu-Buddhist cosmology.

Kumari Ghar

Taleju Temple

Built in 1564, this three-tiered temple dedicated to the royal deity Taleju Bhawani rises 35 meters and opens only during Dashain festival.

Kasthamandap

The "Wooden Pavilion" that gave Kathmandu its name. This 16th-century structure was rebuilt after the 2015 earthquake.

Hanuman Dhoka Palace

The former royal palace complex contains courtyards, museums, and temples dating from the 16th to 19th centuries.

Read more

Jagannath Temple

Famous for its erotic carvings on the roof struts, this 16th-century temple showcases some of the finest woodcarvings in the valley.

Kal Bhairav

A massive stone image of Bhairav, the fearsome manifestation of Shiva, used historically as a place to administer oaths.

Cultural Significance

Living Heritage

Kathmandu Durbar Square is not merely a historical relic but a living cultural space where centuries-old traditions continue to thrive. The square serves as the stage for numerous festivals, religious ceremonies, and daily rituals that connect modern Nepalis to their ancestral past.

The Kumari Jatra festival, part of the Indra Jatra celebration, features the public appearance of the living goddess Kumari, who blesses the gathered crowd from her ornate chariot. This centuries-old tradition exemplifies the continuing spiritual significance of the square.

Throughout the year, the square pulses with life as locals worship at temples, tourists explore the historic structures, and artisans practice traditional crafts in the surrounding streets, making it a true cultural crossroads.

Practical Information

Visiting Tips

  • Entry Fee: NPR 1000 (approximately USD 8) for foreign tourists. Free for Nepali citizens.
  • Opening Hours: The square is open daily from 7 AM to 7 PM.
  • Best Time to Visit: Early morning or late afternoon to avoid crowds and harsh midday sun.
  • Guided Tours: Available at the entrance, offering deeper insights into the history and significance of the structures.
  • Photography: Permitted throughout the square, except inside certain temples.
  • Dress Code: Modest attire is recommended, especially when entering temples (shoulders and knees covered).

Getting There

  • From Thamel (tourist district): 15-minute walk or 5-minute taxi ride.
  • From Tribhuvan International Airport: 7 km away, approximately 30 minutes by taxi.
  • Public Transport: Local buses and tempos (three-wheeled vehicles) run regularly to Durbar Square.

Best Time to Visit Kathmandu

  • October-November: Peak season with clear skies, comfortable temperatures, and major festivals like Dashain and Tihar.
  • March-May: Spring season with pleasant weather and rhododendrons in bloom.
  • December-February: Winter season with cool temperatures but clear mountain views.
  • June-September: Monsoon season with occasional rain but fewer tourists and lush green landscapes.

Culinary Delights

Sample traditional Newari cuisine at restaurants near Durbar Square, including momos (dumplings), sel roti (sweet rice bread), and the festive Newari feast known as "Samay Baji."

Artisanal Crafts

Explore the surrounding lanes to discover workshops where artisans create traditional Nepalese crafts, from wood carving and metal work to thangka painting and pottery.

Festivals

Plan your visit around major festivals like Indra Jatra, Dashain, or Tihar to witness vibrant celebrations that transform Durbar Square into a spectacular cultural showcase.
